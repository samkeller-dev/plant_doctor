# Repotting Houseplants

Repotting refreshes spent soil and gives roots room to grow. It is also commonly mistimed — both performed too often (disturbing settled plants) and too rarely (leaving plants stunted in compacted soil).

## When to repot

Repot when one or more of the following is true:
- Roots circle the inside of the pot or grow out of the drainage holes.
- Water runs straight through without wetting the root ball — the plant is fully root-bound and there is little soil left to absorb water.
- Soil has degraded into fine dust or shrunk significantly from the pot walls.
- The plant has visibly outgrown its pot and topples easily.
- Two to three years have passed for a slow grower; annually for fast growers like pothos or many tropicals.

## When NOT to repot

- Immediately after purchase, unless the plant is in obviously distressed soil. Most plants benefit from 2–4 weeks acclimation first.
- During winter dormancy for temperate-cycle species. Most tropicals tolerate year-round repotting indoors but recover fastest in spring.
- When the plant is already stressed (severe pest infestation, root rot under treatment, recent move). Stabilize first.
- "Just because" — a plant that is healthy and growing in its current pot does not need disturbance.

## How to repot

1. **Choose a pot only 1–2 inches (2–5 cm) larger in diameter** than the current one. Oversized pots hold more soil than the roots can dry, raising root-rot risk.
2. **Use fresh, appropriate potting mix** — general-purpose mix for most foliage plants, fast-draining cactus/succulent mix for succulents, bark-based orchid mix for epiphytes. Garden soil is not appropriate; it compacts and may carry pests.
3. **Remove the plant** by tipping the pot sideways and squeezing or tapping its sides. Do not pull on stems.
4. **Tease the outer roots loose**, especially if they are circling. Cut deeply circling roots; the plant will regrow.
5. **Position the plant so the soil line stays the same**. Burying the stem too deep promotes rot.
6. **Backfill, firm gently, water in.** Watering settles soil around roots and reveals air pockets to top up.

## Recovery

- Some leaf yellowing or wilting in the first 1–2 weeks is normal transplant shock.
- Hold off fertilizer for 4–6 weeks; fresh mix already contains starter nutrients, and damaged roots are sensitive to salts.
- Keep the plant in slightly lower light and out of drafts during the first two weeks.
