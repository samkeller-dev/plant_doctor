# Scale Insects

Scale insects are sap-sucking pests that often go unnoticed until populations are large. Indoor problems are usually soft scales (e.g., brown soft scale, *Coccus hesperidum*) and armored scales, plus mealybugs, which are a soft scale relative.

## Identification

- Small (1–5 mm) immobile bumps on stems, leaf undersides, and along veins. Color ranges from tan and brown to white or gray.
- They look like part of the plant — easily mistaken for bark texture or a natural bump. Scrape gently with a fingernail; scales pop off, leaving a small wound.
- **Sticky leaves or a sticky residue on surfaces below the plant** is a near-certain sign of soft scale or mealybug — this is honeydew, the insects' sugary excretion.
- Black sooty mold may grow on the honeydew.
- Mealybugs appear as fluffy white cottony tufts in leaf axils and on roots.

## Hosts

Scale is common on ficus, citrus, hoya, schefflera, ivy, ferns, and orchids. Mealybugs additionally favor succulents, peperomias, and African violets.

## Treatment

1. **Isolate** the plant.
2. **Physical removal** is the single most effective first step. Wipe each insect with a cotton swab dipped in 70% isopropyl alcohol; the alcohol breaches the waxy coating and kills on contact. For heavy infestations, a soft toothbrush works on stems.
3. **Horticultural oil or insecticidal soap** sprayed weekly for 3–4 weeks suffocates crawlers (the mobile juvenile stage). Adult armored scales are largely protected by their shell; oils still smother eggs and crawlers underneath.
4. **Systemic insecticides** containing imidacloprid or dinotefuran can be drenched into the soil for severe or hard-to-reach infestations. Do not use on edibles or on flowering plants visited by pollinators.
5. **Heavy pruning** of the worst-infested branches accelerates control on woody plants like ficus.

## Why early detection matters

Adult scales are mostly immobile and easy to overlook, but each female can produce hundreds of crawlers that disperse to other plants. Inspect new arrivals before placing them near the rest of the collection.
