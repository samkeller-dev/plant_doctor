# Light Requirements for Common Houseplants

Light is the single most-misjudged variable in indoor growing. Indoor light levels are far lower than outdoor shade — a "bright" indoor location is often dim by horticultural standards. Window orientation, distance from glass, and obstructions all matter as much as the species' preferences.

## A practical light vocabulary

- **Direct sun**: unfiltered sunlight reaches the leaves for at least several hours. A south-facing (Northern Hemisphere) window with no obstructions, within ~3 feet of the glass.
- **Bright indirect**: the brightest light available without the sun's disc striking the leaves. East-facing windows; just to the side of a south or west window; sheer-curtained south exposures.
- **Medium light**: enough light to read a book without a lamp throughout the day. Several feet from a bright window or near a north-facing window with no obstructions.
- **Low light**: the corner of a room with windows, or interior rooms with only ambient light. Most plants tolerate it but few thrive.

## Symptoms of too little light

- Stretched, leggy growth with long internodes (the gap between leaves on the stem).
- Small, pale new leaves; smaller than older leaves on the same plant.
- Variegated cultivars revert toward solid green.
- Slow or no new growth even in active season.
- Soil stays wet for much longer than expected — a low-light plant uses less water.

## Symptoms of too much light

- Bleached, washed-out patches where direct sun strikes leaves, often crispy in the center.
- Leaves angle away from the source or curl downward.
- Foliage becomes pale yellow-green overall (a stress response, not chlorosis).

## Common species summary

- **Snake plant, ZZ, pothos, philodendron**: tolerate low to medium; thrive in bright indirect.
- **Monstera, fiddle-leaf fig, rubber plant**: bright indirect; tolerate some direct morning sun.
- **Calathea, prayer plant, peace lily**: medium to bright indirect; direct sun bleaches them.
- **Succulents, cacti, citrus, herbs**: direct sun; insufficient light is the most common reason these decline indoors.
- **Orchids (Phalaenopsis)**: bright indirect, never direct midday sun.

## When in doubt

Move a struggling plant closer to the window before changing watering or fertilizer. Light is almost always the limiting factor indoors.
