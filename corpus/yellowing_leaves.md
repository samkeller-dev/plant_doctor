# Yellowing Leaves (Chlorosis) in Houseplants

Yellowing of leaves — chlorosis — is one of the most common houseplant complaints and rarely has a single cause. Diagnosis depends on which leaves are yellowing, how quickly, and what other symptoms accompany the discoloration.

## Common patterns

- **Lower / older leaves yellowing uniformly, often dropping**: most often overwatering or natural senescence. If soil is wet and roots are dark/mushy, suspect root rot. If watering is appropriate and only the oldest one or two leaves are affected over weeks, this is normal leaf turnover.
- **New / upper leaves yellow with green veins (interveinal chlorosis)**: typically iron or manganese deficiency, often triggered by alkaline soil or waterlogged roots that prevent nutrient uptake rather than the absence of the nutrient itself.
- **Uniform pale yellowing of all foliage**: nitrogen deficiency, severe light deprivation, or root binding in a long-unrepotted plant.
- **Yellow patches with a sticky residue or webbing**: pest damage — see the spider-mite and scale references.
- **Sudden yellowing after a move or repot**: transplant shock or sudden change in light/temperature/humidity. Resolve over 2–4 weeks if conditions are stable.

## Diagnostic steps

1. Check soil moisture two inches down with a finger or wooden skewer before watering.
2. Lift the pot — light pots usually mean dry soil; unexpectedly heavy pots after several days suggest poor drainage.
3. Inspect undersides of leaves and stem joints for pests.
4. Note recent changes: new location, repotting, fertilizer, season change.

## When to act

Stop watering and inspect roots if soil is consistently wet and yellowing is rapid. For interveinal chlorosis, flush the pot with plain water to leach accumulated salts before reaching for fertilizer. Remove individual yellow leaves only if they are fully yellow — partially yellow leaves still photosynthesize.
