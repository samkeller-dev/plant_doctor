# Spider Mites

Spider mites (most commonly *Tetranychus urticae*, the two-spotted spider mite) are among the most damaging indoor pests. They are not insects — they are arachnids, less than 0.5 mm across, and easy to miss until the population explodes.

## Identification

- Fine pale stippling or "salt-and-pepper" speckling on the upper leaf surface, caused by mites feeding on the underside.
- Webbing in leaf axils, along stems, or between leaves — a hallmark of advanced infestations.
- Tap a leaf over a sheet of white paper; tiny moving dots confirm mites.
- A 10x hand lens shows the mites clearly on the leaf underside, often with translucent eggs.
- Hosts most often hit indoors: ivy, calathea, alocasia, palms, scheffleras, edibles, and any plant under hot, dry conditions.

## Conditions that favor outbreaks

Hot, dry indoor air — particularly near heating vents in winter — accelerates the mite life cycle from weeks to days. Dusty leaves and water-stressed plants are more vulnerable. Spider mites cannot tolerate sustained humidity above ~60%.

## Treatment

1. **Isolate** infested plants immediately; mites spread by air currents and contact.
2. **Physical removal**: rinse the entire plant — especially leaf undersides — under a strong shower or with a sink sprayer. Repeat every 3–5 days for at least three weeks to disrupt the egg-to-adult cycle.
3. **Insecticidal soap or horticultural oil**: spray to runoff, coating leaf undersides. Multiple applications 5–7 days apart are essential because most products do not kill eggs.
4. **Increase humidity** around the plant after treatment with grouping, a pebble tray, or a humidifier.
5. For valuable specimens with persistent infestations, predatory mites (*Phytoseiulus persimilis*) are effective biological controls available from greenhouse suppliers.

## Do not

- Do not use neem oil or horticultural oil on plants in direct sun or in temperatures above ~85 °F (29 °C); leaf burn can result.
- Do not assume one treatment is enough. Failed eradications almost always come from skipping follow-up sprays.
