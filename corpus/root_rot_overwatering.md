# Root Rot and Overwatering

Root rot is the leading cause of houseplant death. The pathogens involved (commonly *Pythium*, *Phytophthora*, *Rhizoctonia*, and *Fusarium* spp.) are opportunistic — they thrive when roots are oxygen-starved in saturated soil. The trigger is almost always cultural (overwatering, poor drainage), not infection alone.

## Symptoms

- Lower leaves yellow and drop quickly.
- Wilting *despite* moist soil — counterintuitive, because rotted roots cannot move water even when it is present.
- A sour or musty smell from the soil surface.
- Stem softens at the soil line and may darken.
- Roots, when unpotted, are brown or black, slimy, and slough off when touched. Healthy roots are firm and white or tan.

## Why "overwatering" is misleading

The damage is not caused by water volume per se but by how long roots sit in saturated soil without air. A plant in a fast-draining mix in a porous pot can be watered daily without harm; the same plant in dense soil in a non-draining cachepot can rot from a single weekly watering. Pot size matters too: an oversized pot holds more wet soil than the small root mass can dry out.

## Recovery

1. Unpot and rinse soil from roots.
2. Cut away all dark, soft roots with clean shears, back to firm white tissue.
3. Repot into fresh, well-draining medium (e.g., potting mix amended 1:1 with perlite or pumice) in a pot with drainage holes, sized only slightly larger than the remaining root mass.
4. Water lightly and place in bright indirect light. Do not fertilize for 4–6 weeks while roots regenerate.
5. If more than ~70% of roots were lost, take cuttings as a backup — recovery is uncertain.

## Prevention

- Drainage holes are non-negotiable. Cachepots without holes accumulate water; empty them after watering.
- Match watering to soil drying, not to a calendar. Most foliage houseplants prefer the top 1–2 inches to dry between waterings.
- Repot bound, compacted, or hydrophobic soil annually for fast growers.
