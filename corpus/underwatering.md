# Underwatering and Drought Stress

Underwatering is less often fatal than overwatering but is frequently misdiagnosed *as* overwatering because the symptoms overlap (wilting, leaf yellowing, leaf drop). The key differentiator is soil condition.

## Symptoms

- Leaves wilt, droop, or curl inward; tropical foliage may appear "limp."
- Crispy brown leaf tips and edges, especially in low humidity.
- Dry, lightweight pot; soil pulls away from the sides.
- In severe cases, soil becomes hydrophobic — water poured on top runs through the gap between soil and pot wall without wetting the root ball.
- Lower leaves may yellow and drop; succulents wrinkle or pucker.

## Underwatering vs. overwatering

| Feature | Underwatered | Overwatered / root rot |
|---|---|---|
| Soil | Dry, light, may be cracked | Wet, heavy, sour smell |
| Leaves | Crispy edges, wilt, then drop | Soft yellowing, wilt despite moisture |
| Roots | Dry but firm and pale | Brown, mushy, slimy |
| Recovery | Usually rapid after deep soak | Slow; requires repotting |

## Recovery

1. **Bottom-water** by setting the pot in a tray of water for 20–30 minutes so the root ball rehydrates evenly. Top-watering hydrophobic soil mostly runs off.
2. After bottom-watering, drain thoroughly. Do not leave the pot sitting in standing water.
3. For severely dried soil, repeat bottom-watering after 24 hours; one soak rarely fully rehydrates a compacted root ball.
4. Trim crispy tips with clean scissors, following the natural leaf shape, for cosmetic recovery only — they will not turn green again.

## Prevention

- Check soil moisture before watering rather than relying on a fixed schedule. Indoor humidity, season, and pot material all change drying rate.
- Terracotta dries faster than glazed ceramic or plastic; account for it.
- For drought-tender species (calathea, ferns, peace lily), a moisture meter or a consistent weekly check is more reliable than memory.
